Cuisine

Dim sum are small dishes like dumplings and buns, traditionally served in teahouses with tea. It's a social dining experience known as “yum cha,” popularized in Hong Kong for brunch gatherings.

Char siu is a barbecued pork marinated in honey, soy sauce, and Chinese five-spice. With roots in southern China, it's become a staple in Hong Kong roast meat shops, often served with rice or inside buns.

Egg tarts were inspired by British custard tarts and Portuguese pastéis de nata, and arrived in Hong Kong in the 1940s. The flaky or buttery crust and smooth egg custard filling made them a beloved bakery snack.

Pineapple bun Despite its name, this bun contains no pineapple. Its sugary, golden crust resembles a pineapple's texture. Originating in post-war Hong Kong, it reflects Western baking influences on Chinese pastries. Often eaten warm with a slice of butter.

Curry fish balls are a classic Hong Kong street food that is simmered in a spicy curry sauce. Popular since the 1950s, they reflect both local tastes and British colonial influence through the use of curry.

Hong Kong milk tea is nicknamed "silk stocking tea" due to the cloth filter used in brewing. This strong black tea mixed with evaporated milk is a legacy of British colonial influence. It's a creamy, slightly bitter drink found in nearly every Hong Kong diner.
